## About the Program & Agency

### DOH Central Visayas CHD
- Regional office of the Department of Health responsible for health services and facilities in Central Visayas.
- Implements national health programs at the regional level.

### HSRP (Health Systems Resilience Project)
- A multi-year program (5 years) aimed at strengthening health systems to withstand emergencies (pandemics, natural disasters, etc.).
- Components include:
  - Upgrading health facilities (civil works)
  - Procurement of equipment
  - Training and capacity building

### Your Role: Project Associate I (Quantity Surveyor)
- Focus on civil works component.
- Duties: cost estimates, tender evaluation, contract management, site monitoring, coordination with HFEP Unit, financial risk assessment.
- Job Order status (renewable based on performance and funding).

## Common Interview Questions (with Answer Pointers)

### 1. "Tell us about yourself."
- **Structure:** Brief introduction → key experiences → current role → why you're here.
- **Sample:**
  > "I'm a licensed Civil Engineer with over 10 years of experience in quantity surveying and project cost management. I started at Cebu South Medical Center, preparing BOQs and monitoring hospital expansions. Then at DSWD Field Office VII, I handled government-funded infrastructure projects—supervising construction, auditing billings, ensuring compliance with national standards. Most recently at King Properties, I led a team of QSs for high-rise developments valued at over 900M PHP. I also developed a financial dashboard that reduced reporting time by 87% and drove value engineering saving 15% on costs. I'm now an independent QS consultant, but I'm eager to bring my technical skills and public service experience back to government through DOH's HSRP."

### 2. "Why do you want to work in government / DOH specifically?"
- Emphasize public service motivation, alignment with DOH's mission, stability of a 5-year program.
- Mention past government experience (DSWD, medical center) and how it gave you fulfillment.
- Show you understand the impact of health infrastructure on communities.

### 3. "What do you know about the HSRP?"
- Demonstrate research: It's a resilience-focused program under HFEP; involves upgrading health facilities; your role supports civil works; you're aware it's a multi-year commitment.

### 4. "How do you prepare a cost estimate?"
- Walk through your process:
  1. Review drawings, specs, geotech reports.
  2. Perform quantity takeoffs (Planswift, manual if needed).
  3. Apply unit rates (market research, DPWH standards).
  4. Factor in labor, materials, equipment, contingencies.
  5. Review with team, adjust based on feedback.
- Mention experience with large-scale commercial/residential projects.

### 5. "How do you handle contract variations?"
- Verify scope change against contract.
- Assess cost/time impact.
- Negotiate with contractor, aiming for fair adjustment.
- Document thoroughly (variation order).
- Update budget and track in dashboard.
- Example from King Properties.

### 6. "Describe a time you had to monitor a construction site."
- Use STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result).
- Example from DSWD: supervised multiple municipal projects; conducted site visits; identified substandard concrete; issued correction notice; ensured rework; project completed to standard.

### 7. "How do you ensure accuracy in your work?"
- Double-check system.
- Peer reviews.
- Use of automated tools (Excel dashboards, checklists).
- Continuous learning from feedback (mention ROBIN award for reducing errors).

### 8. "How do you manage multiple deadlines?"
- Prioritization based on project milestones.
- Use of task management tools (MeisterTask, Notion).
- Regular communication with team.
- Example from King Properties: tight bid deadlines, used structured workflow to deliver on time.

### 9. "Have you worked with government procurement rules (RA 9184)?"
- Yes, at DSWD and Cebu South Medical Center.
- Familiar with public bidding, post-qualification, documentation requirements, and the importance of transparency.

### 10. "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?"
- Growing within the program, taking on more responsibility, mentoring junior staff.
- Contributing to more resilient health infrastructure.
- Possibly moving into project management while maintaining QS expertise.

## Panel Interview Questions (Directors' Level)

These questions may come from the **Regional Director** (strategic, impact-focused) and the **Program Director** (implementation, technical, and team-oriented).

### 11. (Regional Director) "How do you see the role of infrastructure in improving health outcomes?"
> "Infrastructure is the backbone of healthcare delivery. A well-built, resilient health facility ensures that patients receive timely care, that equipment functions properly, and that staff can work in a safe environment. During emergencies, resilient infrastructure means continuity of services—lives depend on it. In my previous work at Cebu South Medical Center, I saw how proper planning and quality construction directly impacted patient capacity and service delivery. I'm committed to ensuring that every peso spent on civil works translates into a facility that truly serves its community."

### 12. (Program Director) "This program involves multiple stakeholders—DOH central office, LGUs, contractors, and communities. How will you ensure effective coordination?"
> "I believe clear communication and documentation are key. I would establish regular coordination meetings, maintain a centralized tracking system for project updates, and ensure that all parties have access to the same information. At King Properties, I led a team of QSs and coordinated with project managers, contractors, and suppliers to keep projects on track. I also learned at DSWD how to navigate multi-stakeholder environments, including LGUs and community volunteers. I'm comfortable bridging technical and non-technical audiences."

### 13. (Regional Director) "What do you think are the biggest challenges in implementing health infrastructure projects in the region, and how would you address them?"
> "From my experience, challenges include delays in fund releases, contractor capacity, and coordination among agencies. To address these, I would advocate for proactive planning—building buffer times into schedules, conducting thorough bid evaluations to select capable contractors, and maintaining open lines with the RPMO and HFEP Unit to anticipate bottlenecks. I also believe that data transparency, like the dashboards I built, helps identify issues early so we can intervene before they become major problems."

### 14. (Program Director) "Describe a time you had to make a difficult decision that balanced cost, quality, and timeline."
> (STAR)
> - **Situation:** At King Properties, a subcontractor proposed a cheaper but lower-quality material for a high-rise project.
> - **Task:** I needed to decide whether to accept the cost savings or insist on the specified quality.
> - **Action:** I analyzed the long-term maintenance costs and risks. I presented a cost-benefit analysis to management, showing that the cheaper material would lead to higher expenses over time. We decided to stick with the original spec, though it meant a slight budget increase.
> - **Result:** The project maintained quality, and we avoided potential rework. Management appreciated the data-driven approach.

### 15. (Regional Director) "How do you handle pressure when multiple projects are at risk of delay?"
> "I stay calm and focus on prioritization. First, I identify which delays are critical and which can be managed. Then I communicate transparently with all stakeholders—contractors, the project team, and management—about the situation and our mitigation plan. At DSWD, I managed several municipal projects simultaneously; when one faced a delay due to weather, I reallocated monitoring time to others and worked with the contractor to adjust schedules. I also use tracking tools to keep everyone informed. Pressure is part of the job, but clear communication and a structured approach keep things under control."

### 16. (Program Director) "This position requires site visits to different islands. How do you prepare for fieldwork, and how do you ensure your office tasks don't suffer?"
> "I plan ahead. Before a site visit, I complete all pending office work or hand over critical tasks to a colleague. I also bring offline tools—printed drawings, spreadsheets—so I can continue analysis during travel. After the visit, I promptly document findings and update the project team. At DSWD, I regularly traveled to remote sites, and I maintained a system that kept my reports on track. I also see site visits as essential to quality assurance; they're not a distraction but a core part of the job."

### 17. (Regional Director) "Why should we choose you over other candidates?"
> "I bring a unique combination of technical QS expertise, proven leadership, and a genuine commitment to public service. My 10+ years of experience span both government and private sector, giving me a well-rounded perspective. I've innovated with data automation to save time and improve accuracy—skills that will help the program run efficiently. But most importantly, I care about the impact of our work. I've seen how health facilities change lives, and I want to dedicate my skills to that mission."

### 18. (Program Director) "If you are hired, what would be your first 30-day plan?"
> "My first 30 days would focus on learning and relationship-building. I would:
> - Meet with the RPMO and HFEP Unit to understand ongoing projects and priorities.
> - Review all active civil works contracts, budgets, and schedules.
> - Familiarize myself with the HSRP guidelines, procurement processes, and reporting requirements.
> - Visit at least one project site to see ground realities.
> - Identify any immediate risks or bottlenecks and propose initial solutions.
> - Establish clear communication channels with my supervisor and team.
> I believe in starting with a solid foundation so I can contribute effectively from day one."

### 19. (Regional Director) "How do you stay motivated when projects face setbacks?"
> "I focus on the bigger picture—the communities that will benefit from the completed facility. Setbacks are temporary; they're opportunities to learn and improve. At King Properties, when a project faced a delay due to design changes, I used the extra time to refine cost estimates and identify value engineering options. I also draw motivation from my team; we support each other and celebrate small wins along the way. Resilience is key, both in infrastructure and in people."
